Review of Les Misérables by Victor Hugo

Les Misérables is one of the most powerful and enduring novels ever written. Victor Hugo’s 19th-century masterpiece explores themes of justice, love, redemption, and the struggle of the human spirit against oppression. Set in post-revolutionary France, the story follows Jean Valjean, a man imprisoned for stealing bread, as he seeks to rebuild his life and find redemption in a society that refuses to forgive him.

Plot and Themes

Hugo weaves together multiple storylines, from the relentless pursuit of Valjean by Inspector Javert to the tragic life of Fantine and her daughter Cosette. Each character embodies a facet of society—its cruelty, compassion, and hope. The novel’s sweeping scope captures the struggles of the poor, the corruption of institutions, and the power of mercy and love to transform even the darkest hearts.

“To love another person is to see the face of God.” — Victor Hugo, Les Misérables

Writing Style

Hugo’s prose is rich and poetic, filled with philosophical reflections on humanity and justice. Though at times lengthy and digressive, his writing gives the novel its grandeur and emotional weight. His ability to combine social criticism with deep empathy makes Les Misérables timeless and universally relevant.

Overall Impression

Les Misérables is not just a novel—it is an emotional and moral journey. It reminds readers that kindness and forgiveness can redeem even the most broken souls. Despite its historical setting, its message about compassion and social justice remains profoundly modern.
